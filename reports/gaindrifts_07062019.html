1. Constant gain mismatches at a given time scale $\tau$¶

In previous posting I've found that the gain estimate procedure worked properly if we injected gain mismatches as a constant fluctuation around 1 and with a certain width ($\sigma_g$ ) on just one detector per detector pair.

The input maps related to those kind of studies encoded mostly the Dipole and CMB fluctuations (only scalar anisotropies). However, we didn't test that procedure when the signal leaking into polarization encoded a larger thermal load: as the CMB Monopole for a satellite.

There are two major changes with what has been shown before: (i) we included a constant offset to the input maps, mimicking the effect of the CMB Monopole, (ii) the gain mismatches are now injected on both detectors in a detector pair, (iii) we high-pass filter the data to get rid of the low freq. signal (due to mostly Dipole and Monopole) and retain just the CMB fluctuations, (iv) we simulated white noise in the TODs by rescaling the equivalent NET by $\sqrt{N_{det}}$.

Below the parameters used in Toast simulations:

  • Noise simulated :True
  • Detector pairs: 2
  • T_obs : 1 yr
  • sampling freq: 20 Hz
  • precession period :96.1803 min
  • spin period: 10 min
  • HWP : False
  • spin angle : 50
  • precession angle :45
  • MADAM destriper (baseline=60 sec)
  • Input maps: Monopole, Dipole, CMB T

We therefore tried several cases by changing the mismatch time scale, $\tau$ and $\sigma_g$.

The procedure to mitigate gain mismatches is performed on each chunck (related to $\tau$) of time samples $d_{t , t+\tau}$ of a given detector:

  1. Apply a high-pass filter to $d_{t , t+\tau} $ with cutoff frequency $f_{c}=10$ mHz
  2. Regress $d_{t , t+\tau} $ against signal-only template $T$ and estimate the regression slope $\hat{g}$ as $$ \hat{g} = \frac { T^{t} d } { T^{t} T } $$
  3. Mitigate the leakage, dividing $d_{t , t+\tau} $ by $\hat{g}$
  4. Estimate maps after mitigation

We 've found that we are able to track the gain mismatches pretty well except when both $\sigma_g$ and $\tau$ are small. To better quantify this, we can estimate the correlation index $\rho$ between the true underlying gain mismatches and the gain estimates. The closer $\rho$ is to 1, the more the estimates are correlated to the true ones, hence the better are the performance of the gain estimates.

Fig2 2D Histrogram of scatter plot of the true gain mismatches vs. the gain estimates. The histograms are not normalized, so that the colorscale indicates how many points fall into each bin. The number of bins is chosen accordingly to the width of gain fluctuations, for top (bottom) panels being $N_{bins}=100 (50)$. AS already stated above, the larger is $\tau $, the more the estimates are correlated with the underlying ones, meaning that is better to estimate the gains on a larger timescale.

summary¶

We found that:

  • When we inject constant gain mismatches into observations encoding Monopole+Dipole+ CMB T, the gain estimates track the underlying gain fluctuations.
  • If we high-pass filter the data, the performances are not related to the time scale we perform the estimate, but rather on the amplitude of the fluctuations.
  • If $\sigma_g $ is smaller or comparable to the filtered signal fluctuations, which are essentially just CMB T anisotropies $\delta T/T $ (as in the case of the bottom panels of fig.2 ), the estimates don't track the underlying gain mismatches. The correlation index represents a good statistical figure of merit of how well the gain estimates are tracking the true mismatches.
  • On the other hand, if the data are not high-pass filtered the gain estimation works for any $\tau$ and $\sigma_g$.

Summary¶

If compared with Fig1 above we notice that we are more able to track the gain drifts with a larger $\sigma_g$ and with a longer $\tau$. As pointed out previously, without highpass filtering the data, the gain estimates tracks perfectly both gain drifts and mismatches (Also, I've checked that the presence of noise doesn't affect the gain estimates)

These results might lead to the following conclusions:

  • Given $\sigma_g$, the signal leaking to polarization comes mostly from Monopole and CMB Dipole $\sim 1$ K , and its amplitude will be proportional to $ \sigma_g $, $\sim \mathcal{O}(\sigma_g) $ K
  • When we High-pass filter the data, we essentially filter all the low frequency signals in the data, and the amplitude of polarized leaked signal is at most at the same order of magnitude of the filtered signal ( i.e. just the CMB Temperature anisotropies ) both on Q and U maps . In the figure below you see indeed that the mitigation technique works fairly well when we increase $\sigma_g$ to the level of $1\%$
  • As a result, the gain estimates are noisy especially when the gain is drifting, or when we estimate the gain on shorter time scales.
