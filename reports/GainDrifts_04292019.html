0. Injecting Gain Drifts¶

We inject drifts with a $1/f$ dependence with $f_{knee}=150 $ mHz (see PSD below) on all detectors within a detector pair .

The input signal encodes: Monopole + Dipole + CMB T fluctuations. So far, no noise is injected to data.

We estimated maps with MADAM destriper (baseline=60 sec)

Mitigating leakage¶

To mitigate the leakage we estimate the gain by regressing the signal against a template encoding the CMB T with $\ell \gtrsim 50$.

Below are the mitigation steps to a generic TOD $d_t$:

  1. Consider a small chunk of $d_t$ corresponding to a given timescale $\tau$ of 10 min.
  2. Apply a high-pass filter to $d_{t , t+\tau} $ with cutoff frequency $f_{c}=10$ mHz $^{(1)}$

  3. Regress $d_{t , t+\tau} $ against a template $T$ and estimate the regression slope $\hat{g}$ as $$ \hat{g} = \frac { T^{t} d } { T^{t} T } $$ We tested three different regression techniques:

    • TOD regr.1 : In this post I want to compare 3 different techniques of regression to check which one is the best in actually tracking the gain drifts on a time scale of $\tau =10 $ min. As you can see this technique relies on fitting against a template TOD, $T$.

    • TOD regr. 2 : We can also find the slope between the two detector pairs TOD by fitting a straight line across each chunk of data. One of the pro of this choice is the fact that we don't rely on a template to fit the gain drifts, but it might be strongly affected by noise. I chose as a low frequency band the range between 10 to 200 mHz.

    • PSD regr. : The third rel. gain correction technique I've studied is described in this post by Sinan and it relies on fitting the gain variations as the ratio between the low frequency band amplitudes of the cross spectrum diff x sum and PSD of pair sums.

  4. The gain correction $d_{t , t+\tau} $ is then applied in the TOD of each detector : $$ \tilde{d} _{t , t+\tau} = d_{t , t+\tau} / \hat{g} $$

I've compared the performances of the three mitigation techniques, by changing the input signals. The sections below refers to three cases starting from an input signal including Monopole+ Dipole+ CMB T to a simple input signal with just the high frequency signal, i.e. CMB T. Each section show the case with and without the High Pass filter (HPF) .

Since the T-P leakage in the PairDiff is proportional to the gain mismatches, defined as $\delta g = (g^A - g^B)/2$, we plot this quantity and check which technique is able to reconstruct the gain variations as a function of time.

$^{(1)}$ Given an $\ell_c$ (to retain only signal @ $\ell> \ell_c$ ) we estimate $ f_c$ as : $$ \ell_c = 2 \pi f_c/ \omega_{scan} $$ $\omega_{scan} $ is the scanning speed and it 's defined as : $$ \omega_{scan} = \sin (\beta ) * spinrate = 0.0013 rad/sec =0.07 deg/sec$$ Where $\beta$ is the angle between the boresight and the spin axis (50 deg according to latest values) , and the spin rate is 0.1 rpm .

thus, if $\ell_c=50$ , $f_c= 10$ mHz

for i in range(10): 
    dA = wdrift[i, 0,:] ; dB = wdrift[i, 1,:]; T=wsig[i,0,:]                      
    g = ( gains [0, i*N:(i+1)*N ]  - gains[1, i*N :(i+1)*N ] )/2                        
    alpha0= pl.dot(dA , T) / pl.norm(T)**2                                        
    alpha1= pl.dot(dB , T) / pl.norm(T)**2                                        
    alpha= (alpha0 -alpha1)/2                                                          
    p0 =polyfit(dA, dB, deg=1)[0]                                                      
    t =pl.arange(i*N ,(i+1)*N )/fs  /60

4. Summary¶

  • The cases we analyzed in this posting show that several regression techniques can be exploited to mitigate T-P leakage due to gain drifts. The performances depends on what kind of signal is input and how the TODs are preprocessed before estimating the gains.
  • Two of the 3 techniques don't rely on a template to be fitted against the data.
  • The smaller is the signal the more visible is the effect due to the mitigation.
  • Even though gain drifts show frequency variations on a large range of frequencies (from 0.01 to 20 Hz). Perhaps we should low-pass filter the gain timestream to prevent this high freq. variations, which I think are the responsible of biasing the gain estimates in fig.1.2.
