1. Summary of latest results¶

08/08/2019

In my previous posting, I've chosen 3 parameters needed to inject gain systematics and 2 parameters to mitigate them into our TOAST simulations: $(f_k, f_{lpf} , f_c, \tau_g, \sigma_g ) $, $f_k$ is the knee freq. for the 1/f dependence of gain drift, $f_{lpf}$ the low freq. cutoff of the drift, $\sigma_g$ is the amplitude of the fluctuations, $\tau_g$ the time scale adopted to estimate the gain by regressing the TOD of each detector by a template, $f_c$ high freq. cut-off in case we want to high pass filter the data.

I've shown that the regression technique was able to track both gain mismatches ( i.e. random fluctuations constant during a given timescale $\tau_g$ ) and gain drifts ( slowly varying drifting signal with a $1/f$ dependence ) if :

  • we high pass filter the data with a low frequency cut-off $f_c > f_{\tau} = \tau_g^{-1} $
  • for signal only sims the gain estimates don't depend on $\sigma_g> 10^{-4} $ ( even for signal only sims )

The next step forward is to add noise to the signal and assess how it affects the gain estimates.

Simulation setups¶

Drift Parameters:

  • $f_c = 50$ mHz
  • $ \sigma_g= 10^{-6}$
  • $\tau_g = 1, 10 $ min
  • the gain drift has a 1/f dependence ( with $ f_k = 150 $ mHz ) and it has been low pass filtered with a cut off freq. of 1 mHz, so that all the gain fluctuations below the ~ 10 minutes time scale are filtered out.

Toast Params ( values from latest Litebird-IMO wiki page:

  • Detector pairs: 2
  • frequency band: 90 GHz
  • T_obs : 1 yr
  • sampling freq: 20 Hz
  • precession period :96.1803 min
  • spin period: 10 min
  • Noise simulated : white
  • NET : 30 uK sqrt(s)
  • HWP : False
  • spin angle : 50
  • precession angle :45
  • MADAM destriper (baseline=60 sec)
  • Input maps: Monopole, Dipole, CMB T , Galactic emission @90 GHz

The procedure to mitigate gain mismatches is performed on each chunck (related to $\tau$) of time samples $d_{t , t+\tau}$ of a given detector:

  1. Apply a high-pass filter to $d_{t , t+\tau} $ with cutoff frequency $f_{c}=50$ mHz
    1. Regress $d_{t , t+\tau} $ against signal-only template $T$ and estimate the regression slope $\hat{g}$ as $$ \hat{g} = \frac { T^{t} d } { T^{t} T } $$
    2. Mitigate the leakage, dividing $d_{t , t+\tau} $ by $\hat{g}$
    3. Estimate maps after mitigation

def band_pass_filter(fcutoff, fsampl, data, filtermode='high' ):
    alpha=2. *fcutoff  /fsampl
    padlength = np.int_( fsampl/fcutoff )
    b, a = signal.butter(4, alpha , filtermode)
    filtsignal=  signal.filtfilt(b, a, data,padtype='constant',  padlen=padlength  )
    return filtsignal

def bin_gains(tau, fs,obs,  g) : 
    sizech = pl.int_( tau *fs)  ; nch =pl.int_(  obs *fs*3600  /sizech) 
    gb = pl.zeros(nch)
    for i in range (nch): 
        gb[i] =g[slice(i*sizech, (i+1)*sizech)] .mean()
    return gb

Fig3 Correlation coefficient between gain estimates and binned underlying gain drifts as a function of $\sigma_g$, for the case without (left) and with (right) high-pass filter.

As it can be noticed in figg 1,2,3, the effect of noise on the gain estimate is highly dependent by $\sigma_g$, i.e. the amplitude of gain fluctuations, the lower the noisier are the estimates and the less we are able to track the underlying fluctuations. This is mostly observed when we regress the high-pass filtered data against CMB T anisotropies, because the signal-to-noise ratio is not very high to extract the gain drift with $\sigma_g<10^{-2}$ from a noisy signal. Notice that we see a similar effects on unfiltered data, though on a lower amplitude, i.e. $\sigma_g<10^{-6}$.

2. Assessing effects at TOD level¶

We therefore focus on a fairly low $\sigma_g=10^{-4}$ and check the effect on the TOD data before and after the gain mitigation. As a figure of merit, we estimate the fractional difference $\Delta$ defined as: $$ \Delta = \frac{d-s}{s} $$ where $s$ is the input signal and $d$ is the detector TOD, encoding signal, gain systematics and noise.

3. Summary¶

  • We 've tested our systematics assessment including white noise in our simulations
  • High-pass filtered data are very much affected by noise for small values of $\sigma_g $, < 1%. Vice versa without high pass filtering the TODs we can track gain drifts even at very small fluctuations $\sigma_g< 10^{-6}$.
  • The mitigation reduces the $f_{knee}$ so that it makes negligible the impact of 1/f noise due to gain drift.
