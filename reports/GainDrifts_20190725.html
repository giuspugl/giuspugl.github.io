1. Summary of latest results¶

07/25/2019

In my previous posting, I've shown that the regression technique wasn't able to track both gain mismatches ( i.e. random fluctuations constant during a given timescale $\tau_g$ ) and gain drifts ( slowly varying drifting signal with a $1/f$ dependence ) if :

  • $\tau_g$ was very short ($< 10$ min)
  • we high pass filter the data with a low frequency cut-off $f_c = 1 $mHz
  • $\sigma_g> 10^{-4} $ ( even for signal only sims ) Our conclusions were: _the longer the timescale $ \tau_g $ and the larger $\sigma_g$ the better are the gain estimates_ .

However, we observed some inconsistency in tracking the gains especially when $\tau_g=1$ min. Moreover, for signal only sims we should be able to track very well the gain estimates for any value of $\sigma_g$.

To check whether there was a bug in the gain estimation code I've therefore turned off the gain drifts, high-pass filtered the tod and run the gain estimates and as expected all of them were exactly 1.

I 've also checked the way I filter the data. I use the external python module scipy.signal.filtfilt and found that's very useful to pad the edges of the timestream with a number of samples proportional to the number of samples at my cutoff frequency, i.e. $f_s/f_c$ , where $f_s=20 $ Hz is the sampling rate.

Cutoff frequency plays a crucial role¶

The reason of the inconsistency outlined in the previous postings, is due to the fact that I was filtering the data with a too low cut off frequency. As rule of thumbs we should have :

$$ f_c > f_{\tau} = \tau_g^{-1} $$

In facts, both for $\tau_g = 1 $ and $10 $ min we had respectively $f_{\tau} = 16 $ and 1.6 mHz . In the plots below I show the case with $f_c = 50 $ mHz and you can see how nicely we are able to track the gain estimates for both $\tau_g=1$ and 10 min.

Furthermore, this doesn't depend on the amplitude of the gain fluctuations, I've checked even for very small and large values of $\sigma_g$, the case below encode $\sigma_g = 10^{-6} $ (fairly small wrt previous runs).

Finally, we remind that the gain drift has a 1/f dependence ( with $ f_k = 150 $ mHz ) and it has been low pass filtered with $f_{lpf} = 1 $ mHz, so that all the gain fluctuations below the ~ 10 minutes time scale are filtered out.

To summarize : gain drift systematics are now fully parametrized by 5 parameters $(f_k, f_{lpf} , f_c, \tau_g, \sigma_g ) $

Simulation setups¶

Drift Parameters:

  • $f_c = 50$ mHz
  • $ \sigma_g= 10^{-6}$
  • $\tau_g = 1, 10 $ min
  • the gain drift has a 1/f dependence ( with $ f_k = 150 $ mHz ) and it has been low pass filtered with a cut off freq. of 1 mHz, so that all the gain fluctuations below the ~ 10 minutes time scale are filtered out.

Toast Params:

  • Noise simulated :False
  • Detector pairs: 2
  • T_obs : 1 yr
  • sampling freq: 20 Hz
  • precession period :96.1803 min
  • spin period: 10 min
  • HWP : False
  • spin angle : 50
  • precession angle :45
  • MADAM destriper (baseline=60 sec)
  • Input maps: Monopole, Dipole, CMB T , Galactic emission @ 110 GHz

The procedure to mitigate gain mismatches is performed on each chunck (related to $\tau$) of time samples $d_{t , t+\tau}$ of a given detector:

  1. Apply a high-pass filter to $d_{t , t+\tau} $ with cutoff frequency $f_{c}=50$ mHz
  2. Regress $d_{t , t+\tau} $ against signal-only template $T$ and estimate the regression slope $\hat{g}$ as $$ \hat{g} = \frac { T^{t} d } { T^{t} T } $$
  3. Mitigate the leakage, dividing $d_{t , t+\tau} $ by $\hat{g}$
  4. Estimate maps after mitigation

def bin_gains(tau, fs,obs,  g) : 
    sizech = pl.int_( tau *fs)  ; nch =pl.int_(  obs *fs*3600  /sizech) 
    gb = pl.zeros(nch)
    for i in range (nch): 
        gb[i] =g[slice(i*sizech, (i+1)*sizech)] .mean()
    return gb
